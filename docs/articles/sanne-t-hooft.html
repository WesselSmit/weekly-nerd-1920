Three definitions of micro-interaction by Dan Saffer

  • A signature moment in a digital product
  • A product based around one micro-interaction
  • People see interaction with a digital product as a series of micro-interactions

Micro-interactions are used to make website memorable & pleasurable, micro-interactions should improve the user experience and so it's very important to know who the enduser is and understand their needs.

Micro-interactions

The most important factor in micro-interactions is timing, specifically "sense of movement". Micro-interactions should not last too long because it then becomes tedious and forces the user to wait. "Sense of movement" is how the course of a micro-interaction feels; it's also called the easing. According to Sanne you should never use a linear easing because it looks unnatural, in nature there is nothing that goes exactly linear and so it will always feel like it was made with a computer (which of course is true).

Personally I don't agree with Sanne. First of all there are definitely cases for linear easings, sometimes something is meant to feel robotic / computer made. Secondly, there most certainly are things in nature that are linear; it actually is how we measure time. The very definition of a second is determined by atoms emitting energy, in fact it's so reliable we've been using it as the standard for a second since 1956.

Examples

Most of the college was Sanne explaining how he made a carousel generator with a lot of micro-interactions and him showcasing some micro-interaction rich projects from his freshman students. The goal of showing all these different projects was for them to serve as (not very) subtle examples of how you can improve the user experience using micro-interactions.

Sanne has a website wehere you can generate carousels. If you adjust the properties of the carousel panels, the associated icons also change in the interface. For example, you can keep the corners rounded or sharp, the icons in the interface also get rounded / sharp corners. These are small changes that don't add much; after all, the functionality is the same. But the user experience is better and it is more fun to use the application.

Sanne also showed the work of a handful of first year students, at first I was very impressed with their animation skills because some of the animations looked superb. However, it later turned out that the animations were made in dreamwaver and later exported to CSS. Still very cool to see what was made and a nice source of inspiration about how small micro-interactions can improve your websites.
